# FLN Hub — Pedagogy Reference Guide
**Source:** TaRL (Teaching at the Right Level) Manuals — Pratham India  
**Compiled from:** Level 1 & 2 Language and Math Manuals (Marathi + Hindi), Activity Charts, Worksheet Cards, Story Collections

---

## 1. Core Philosophy: TaRL (Teaching at the Right Level)

The foundational insight of TaRL is simple but radical:

> **A child's grade in school does not reflect their learning level.**

A Class 5 student may read at a Class 1 level. A Class 3 student may be unable to count to 10. The TaRL approach stops pretending otherwise. Instead of moving every child through the same curriculum at the same pace, it:

- **Assesses** where each child actually is (using ASER tools)
- **Groups** children by actual learning level (not grade)
- **Teaches** at that level using activities, games, and guided group work
- **Moves** children forward until they reach grade-level competency

**Post-COVID Context:** The approach explicitly acknowledges that the COVID-19 disruption significantly widened learning gaps. Children moved up grades without consolidating foundational skills. The entire intervention is designed to address this reality.

## 2. ASER Level Definitions — What Each Level Actually Means

### Literacy (Marathi / Hindi Reading)

| Level | ASER Label | What the Child Can Actually Do |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Beginner | Cannot recognize letters; confuses similar-looking characters |
| 1 | Letter | Identifies individual letters; cannot read words fluently; may know letter names but not sounds |
| 2 | Word | Reads simple 2–3 letter words; can decode but not fluently; reads letter-by-letter |
| 3 | Paragraph | Reads a Class 1–level paragraph aloud; pace may be slow; understands some meaning |
| 4 | Story | Reads a Class 2–level story fluently; reads with expression and pace; understands content |

**Reading = Decoding + Comprehension.** The manuals are explicit: fluent reading is only the first step. True reading means understanding content, connecting it to prior knowledge, and discussing it meaningfully.

### Numeracy (Arithmetic)

| Level | ASER Label | What the Child Can Actually Do |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Beginner | Cannot recognize single-digit numbers; cannot count objects reliably |
| 1 | Number 1–9 | Recognizes and writes numbers 1–9; can count objects up to 9 |
| 2 | Number 10–99 | Recognizes and reads 2-digit numbers; understands tens and units; basic skip counting |
| 3 | Subtraction | Performs 2-digit subtraction with regrouping (borrowing); understands place value up to hundreds |
| 4 | Division | Performs division with 2-digit numbers; understands fair sharing; connects division to multiplication |

**Numeracy = Understanding + Fluency.** Children are not drilled for speed. They are expected to estimate first, use multiple methods, and explain their reasoning.

## 3. The 90-Minute Daily Flow

This is the core operational structure that teachers follow every day. The FLN Hub is designed to support and enhance this flow.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              90-MINUTE DAILY FLN SESSION                 │
├──────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┤
│   LITERACY BLOCK (45m)   │   NUMERACY BLOCK (45m)       │
├──────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────┤
│ 15m — Story Discussion   │ 10m — Daily Life Math Talk   │
│ 10m — Vocabulary/Mindmap │ 10m — Number Games/Chanting  │
│ 10m — Role Play / Write  │ 15m — Word Problem Solving   │
│ 10m — Worksheet          │ 10m — Small Group Tasks      │
└──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────┘
```

### Phase 1: Individual Manipulatives (Level-Based Practice)
Each child works at their own level. Activities are selected based on ASER assessment results. A Level 1 child works on letter-sound activities. A Level 3 child works on paragraph comprehension.

### Phase 2: Group Games
Groups of 5–6 children work together on a shared activity. Groups are **level-homogeneous** — all children in the group are at the same ASER level. The teacher rotates between groups, facilitating, asking questions, never lecturing.

### Phase 3: 2v2 Battle
Two students at the **same level** compete in a structured game. This phase is motivational and social — it creates energy and peer accountability. The teacher records who wins and tracks it over time.

---

## 4. Grouping Strategy

- **Group size:** 5–6 children (never more, never less; this is specific and intentional)
- **Grouping basis:** Same ASER level (not grade, not age)
- **Duration per group:** One story/unit for 2 days, then rotation
- **Role rotation:** Within a group, leadership roles change — no one child dominates
- **Teacher circulation:** Teacher rotates through all groups every session; no group is left unattended for too long

**Key principle:** Heterogeneous grades within homogeneous levels. A Class 3 and Class 5 child at the same ASER level will work in the same group. This is intentional — it removes grade-based shame and focuses on competency.

## 5. Specific Activities — Detailed Execution

### Literacy Activities

#### A. Story Discussion (15 minutes, Day 1)
- Groups of 5–6 read the same story together
- Children identify unfamiliar words first (don't skip them, investigate them)
- Each child attempts to understand the word's meaning individually
- Group discussion: What does this word mean? How did you figure it out?
- Read aloud with expression and appropriate pace — not just decoding
- Each group creates 2–3 questions based on the story
- Groups exchange questions; other groups answer and discuss

#### B. Mind Mapping / Vocabulary Building (10 minutes)
- Teacher gives one central word from the story (e.g., "बाजार" / "market")
- Children call out every word or idea that word brings to mind
- All words written on the board (nothing rejected)
- Words grouped by category or relationship
- Children create sentences using 3 of the new words
- Best sentences shared aloud → class vocabulary grows

#### C. Role Play (10 minutes, Day 2)
- Groups assign characters and practice dialogue at home (homework from Day 1)
- 2–3 groups perform daily; each performance max 3–4 minutes
- Other groups watch and give feedback
- Teacher affirms effort, offers one specific improvement suggestion
- Purpose: Confidence, expression, connecting story to real experience

#### D. Writing (10 minutes)
- Topic comes from the story or role play discussion
- Types: picture-based, situational, personal experience, sentence completion
- Teacher circulates, asks "What are you writing? Why?"
- Children self-check first after finishing
- Teacher checks for: (1) Content (are ideas logical and complete?) (2) Structure (correct sentences, punctuation, matras?)
- Children correct their own work with teacher guidance

#### E. Worksheet (10 minutes)
- Small group discusses all questions first (5–6 children together)
- Then each child writes their individual answers
- Self-check after completion
- Teacher discusses answers with the group; corrections are guided, not just given

### Numeracy Activities

#### A. Daily Life Math Discussion (10 minutes)
Real scenarios from the child's world:
- "Anuraj took ₹500 to the fair. He spent ₹160 on sweets, ₹100 on toys. How much remains?"
- "One ball costs ₹24. Sarla needs 3 balls. What will she pay?"
- Multiple correct methods are always accepted and celebrated
- Children explain their method; teacher asks "Can anyone solve it differently?"

#### B. Number Games and Chart Chanting (10 minutes)
**Chanting patterns (minimum 3 days per pattern):**
1. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... (simple counting)
2. 1, 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000... (place value powers)
3. 10×1=10, 10×2=20, 10×3=30... (multiplication as skip-counting)
4. 100, 200, 300, 400... (skip-counting by hundreds)

**Number Diary game:**
- Child A calls out a number
- Child B writes it in their notebook
- Roles alternate
- Builds: number recognition + writing fluency

**Pattern completion:**
- 200, 400, 600, ___, ___
- 300, 800, 1300, ___, ___
- Children call out next numbers

#### C. Word Problem Solving (15 minutes)
Structure is always:
1. **Estimate first** — "What do you think the answer will be roughly?"
2. **Identify** — What information is given? What is asked? What operation?
3. **Solve** — Using place value table (Hundreds | Tens | Units)
4. **Discuss** — How did different groups solve it?
5. **Celebrate** — Multiple correct methods are all valid

#### D. Small Group Tasks — Real-World Math (10 minutes)
Examples of authentic tasks:
- Create a weekly household shopping bill (items, quantities, weight, cost)
- Calculate midday meal ingredients for the school for one day
- Estimate then measure lengths/widths of nearby objects; compare estimates
- Fraction task: "You have 1 roti. Share equally among 2, 3, and 4 children. Draw it."
- Time: "How long does each person take to reach school? What is the total?"

## 7. Teacher's Role — The "Sherpa" Model

The manuals use the metaphor of a Sherpa guide (not a lecturer). The teacher:

**Does:**
- Circulates between groups constantly
- Asks guiding questions: "How did you figure that out? Could it be solved differently?"
- Celebrates effort AND multiple correct methods
- Gives one specific improvement suggestion per feedback
- Ensures every child in every group participates (not just the confident ones)
- Designs activities that connect to children's real lives
- Models good questions, not correct answers

**Does not:**
- Stand at the front and deliver content
- Correct children publicly in a way that causes shame
- Allow one or two children to dominate group work
- Accept only one method of solving a problem
- Test children formally — assessment is observational and conversational

## 8. Assessment Approach

**No formal tests.** Assessment is continuous, embedded, and conversational.

### For Literacy:
- **Reading Level:** Can the child read at appropriate pace? With expression? Does their reading show understanding?
- **Vocabulary:** Does the child use new words appropriately in discussion and writing?
- **Writing:** Two dimensions — (1) Content: Are ideas complete and logical? (2) Structure: Correct sentence structure, punctuation, matras?
- **Comprehension:** During story discussion, can the child connect content to prior knowledge and personal experience?

### For Numeracy:
- **Number Recognition:** Can the child identify and write numbers in the target range?
- **Place Value:** Can the child explain what each digit in a number represents?
- **Problem Solving:** Does the child estimate first? Use manipulatives independently? Try multiple methods?
- **Operations:** Does the child understand the *concept* of the operation (not just the procedure)?

### ASER as Snapshot:
ASER assessment (done at Baseline, Midline, Endline) is a snapshot of where a child is at a point in time. It is not a test — it is a tool to inform grouping and activity selection. The goal is to see children move up levels over the three terms.

## 9. Content: Marathi Language Progression

Based on the Level 1 and Level 2 Marathi manuals (image-based, inferred from pedagogy):

### Level 1 (Letter / Word)
**Letters taught:** अ आ इ ई उ ऊ ए ऐ ओ औ ऋ (vowels) + all consonants (क ख ग घ ङ च छ ज झ ञ ट ठ ड ढ ण त थ द ध न प फ ब भ म य र ल व श ष स ह ळ)

**High-frequency Marathi words (child's world):**
आई, बाबा, घर, शाळा, पाणी, भात, दूध, झाड, फूल, पक्षी, मासा, कुत्रा, मांजर, सूर्य, चंद्र, आकाश, नदी, डोंगर, रस्ता, दुकान, शेत, गाव, बाजार, उन्हाळा, पाऊस, हिवाळा

**Word-to-meaning activities use children's immediate environment:** family, school, village, nature, market, seasons.

### Level 2 (Paragraph / Story)
**Story types:** 2-line stories, paragraph stories, 1-page stories with illustration
**Comprehension activities:** Question creation, cross-group questioning, role play from story
**Vocabulary expansion:** Mind mapping, sentence creation, writing from story prompts

## 10. Content: Numeracy Progression (Detailed)

### From Concrete → Representational → Abstract

Every numeracy concept follows this three-stage progression:
1. **Concrete:** Physical objects (sticks, currency notes, beans, pebbles)
2. **Representational:** Drawings, tables, charts
3. **Abstract:** Symbols and equations

| Concept | Concrete Phase | Abstract Phase |
|---|---|---|
| Place Value | Currency notes of different denominations | Hundreds-Tens-Units table |
| Addition | Combining groups of sticks | Column addition with regrouping |
| Subtraction | Removing sticks from a pile | Borrowing across columns |
| Multiplication | Equal groups of sticks | Times tables, column multiplication |
| Division | Sharing sticks equally in a group | Long division, inverse of multiplication |

### Estimation as a Non-Negotiable Habit
Before every word problem, children estimate the answer. This is not optional — it is the first step every time. It develops number sense and prevents mechanical calculation without understanding.

### Multiple Methods are Always Celebrated
For 24 × 3, acceptable methods include:
- (20 + 4) × 3 = 60 + 12 = 72
- 24 + 24 + 24 = 72
- Place value table: tens × 3 + units × 3

A child who arrives at the right answer a different way has demonstrated deeper understanding, not a mistake.

## 11. Implications for FLN Hub Development

The following principles should guide every feature built or improved:

### 11.1 Every game/simulation should be tagged to a specific ASER level AND explain what that level means
Don't just say "Level: Word". Say: "For students who can read letters but not full words yet."

### 11.2 The 90-minute flow should be visible to teachers
When a teacher logs in, they should see: "Today's session → Individual Practice (Level 2) → Group Game → Battle". The app should make the structure obvious.

### 11.3 Games should suggest what to do if a student is struggling
Brief teacher notes: "If the student can't read this word, go back to letter-by-letter sounding." This is the "one specific improvement suggestion" principle.

### 11.4 Grouping in battles must enforce same-level matching
The matchmaker is not a convenience feature — it is a core pedagogical requirement. Two students at different levels competing is pedagogically harmful (demotivating for the weaker student). This must never be bypassed.

### 11.5 Multiple correct answers should be celebrated, not penalized
In numeracy games, if a student arrives at the right answer using a non-standard method, the game should still celebrate it. Avoid designs that only accept one input method.

### 11.6 Results feed back into assessment
Every battle win, every worksheet completed, every level passed should be visible in the dashboard. Teachers use this data to decide next day's groupings. Assessment → Activity → Assessment is the loop.

### 11.7 Language of the app should match the teacher's world
Field teachers in Maharashtra read Marathi. Instructions, level labels, and game instructions should be bilingual (Marathi + English) at minimum. Never use jargon ("literacyLevel: 3") — say "Paragraph level" or "परिच्छेद स्तर".
